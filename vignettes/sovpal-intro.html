Introduction to sovpal

library(sovpal)
has_ggplot2 <- requireNamespace("ggplot2", quietly = TRUE)

Introduction

The sovpal package provides color palettes derived from Soviet industrial standards and military paint specifications. The palettes span qualitative, sequential, and diverging types, with full ggplot2 integration.

Design Philosophy

The standards-based palettes (gost14202, gost_signal, soviet_military, steppe, hazard) carry fixed hex values traceable to specific documents — GOST paint card numbers, paint manufacturer references, or military paint standards. Modifying these values would alter the historical record.

The constructivist palette is evocative rather than standards-derived; its colors are not fixed in the same sense.

Palettes come in two tiers. Archival palettes are faithful to their source. Visualization-optimized palettes (suffixed _lines, _core, _cvd) select or reorder colors that already appear in an archival palette for distinctness, white-background contrast, or colorblind safety — they never introduce a new hex value. To slice or subset further, index the returned named vector with base R, e.g. sovpal("gost14202")[c("water", "fire")].

hazard — Safe to Danger (Diverging)

show_palette("hazard")

#> All stops are adequate on white backgrounds.
#> Diverging safe-to-danger scale for risk scores, hazard ratios, and survival data: green (safe/low) to grey (neutral) to red (danger/high).

Visualization-optimized palettes

These reuse only canonical hex values from the archival palettes above.

show_palette("gost14202_lines")

#> All stops are adequate for points and lines on white.
#> Lines/points on white backgrounds. Six maximally distinct gost14202 stops.
show_palette("constructivist_core")

#> All stops are adequate on white backgrounds.
#> White-safe Constructivist palette (cream ground removed).
show_palette("hazard_cvd")

#> The light cream midpoint is near-white; it reads as a zero-point in a gradient but is not meant as a standalone discrete swatch.
#> Colorblind-safe diverging safe-to-danger scale: blue (safe/low) to cream (neutral) to red (danger/high). Use in place of `hazard` when red-green color vision deficiency is a concern.
#> Distinguishable under deuteranopia and protanopia.
show_palette("hazard_warm")

#> The light cream midpoint is near-white; it reads as a zero-point in a gradient but is not meant as a standalone discrete swatch.
#> Diverging safe-to-danger scale with a light cream midpoint: green (safe/low) to cream (neutral) to red (danger/high). The most legible and attractive diverging option when colorblind safety is not required.
#> Green-red ends are hard to read under red-green color vision deficiency (~8% of men). Use `hazard_cvd` for those audiences.

White-Background and Colorblind-Safe Usage

Rather than altering colors at runtime, sovpal ships explicit visualization-optimized palettes that reuse only canonical hex values. Request them by name.

Color scales: pick a lines palette

For points and lines on white, use the _lines palette. The light yellow and dark brown stops of gost14202 are dropped — and the choice is visible in the call, not hidden:

library(ggplot2)

ggplot(iris, aes(Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, colour = Species)) +
  geom_point(size = 2) +
  scale_color_sovpal("gost14202_lines") +
  theme_minimal()

Fill scales

scale_fill_sovpal() uses the named palette exactly as defined. For Constructivist on white, use constructivist_core, which omits the low-contrast cream ground:

ggplot(iris, aes(Species, Sepal.Length, fill = Species)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  scale_fill_sovpal("constructivist_core") +
  theme_minimal()

Checking contrast

palette_info() reports each color’s WCAG contrast ratio against white and flags any below the 3:1 non-text threshold:

info <- palette_info("constructivist")
info$contrast_white
#> [1]  5.44 17.40  1.97  1.33  5.75
info$low_contrast_on_white
#> [1] "yellow" "cream"

Using sovpal() directly

sovpal() returns a plain named vector, so base-R indexing covers any custom selection or slice:

# Select colors by name
sovpal("gost14202")[c("water", "fire", "air", "toxic", "alkali", "other")]
#>     water      fire       air     toxic    alkali     other 
#> "#2E7D32" "#C62828" "#5B8DB8" "#E64A19" "#8E6EAF" "#78909C"

# Or just use the ready-made lines palette
sovpal("gost14202_lines")
#>     water      fire       air     toxic    alkali     other 
#> "#2E7D32" "#C62828" "#5B8DB8" "#E64A19" "#8E6EAF" "#78909C"

ggplot2 Integration

library(ggplot2)

df_fluid <- data.frame(
  fluid = factor(names(sovpal("gost14202")), levels = names(sovpal("gost14202"))),
  flow  = c(120, 95, 60, 80, 45, 70, 110, 55)
)

ggplot(df_fluid, aes(fluid, flow, fill = fluid)) +
  geom_col() +
  scale_fill_sovpal("gost14202") +
  labs(title = "GOST 14202-69: Pipeline Colors", x = NULL, y = "Flow rate (L/min)") +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(legend.position = "none", axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 30, hjust = 1))

ggplot(iris, aes(Sepal.Length, Sepal.Width, colour = Species)) +
  geom_point(size = 2.5) +
  scale_color_sovpal("soviet_military") +
  labs(title = "soviet_military") +
  theme_minimal()

# Hazard diverging palette -- risk scores, hazard ratios, survival data
m <- outer(1:10, 1:10, function(x, y) (x - 5.5) * (y - 5.5))
df_hm <- data.frame(
  x = rep(1:10, 10),
  y = rep(1:10, each = 10),
  z = as.vector(m)
)
ggplot(df_hm, aes(x, y, fill = z)) +
  geom_tile() +
  scale_fill_sovpal("hazard", discrete = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "Hazard Palette -- Diverging Risk Heatmap", fill = "Score") +
  theme_minimal() +
  coord_equal()

The diverging palettes use a light cream midpoint that reads as a zero-point and makes both arms pop. hazard itself keeps its archival GOST green/grey/red. For plots, hazard_warm (green/cream/red) is the most legible, and hazard_cvd (blue/cream/red) carries the same safe-to-danger semantics with colorblind-safe hues for red-green-deficient audiences:

ggplot(df_hm, aes(x, y, fill = z)) +
  geom_tile() +
  scale_fill_sovpal("hazard_cvd", discrete = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "hazard_cvd -- Colorblind-Safe Diverging", fill = "Score") +
  theme_minimal() +
  coord_equal()

Historical Notes

GOST 14202-69 — Pipeline Identification

GOST 14202-69 (Truboprovody promyshlennykh predpriyatiy. Opoznavatelnaya okraska, preduprezhdayushchie znaki i markirovochnye shchitki) was adopted in 1969 and standardized the color-coding of industrial pipelines across all Soviet enterprises. The standard specified eight base colors corresponding to fluid categories, referenced by paint card number. The gost14202 hex values correspond to those card-indexed samples. The system remains mandatory in post-Soviet industrial facilities operating under inherited Soviet technical regulations.

GOST 12.4.026 — Safety Signage

GOST 12.4.026 standardized workplace safety signage colors across the Soviet Union. The four signal colors encode hierarchical urgency: red (immediate danger, prohibition), yellow (caution, warning zones), green (safe conditions, evacuation routes), and blue (mandatory action, PPE requirements). Post-Soviet revisions preserved all four signal colors with minimal change.

4BO Protective Green

4BO (Zashchitnyy) is the canonical Soviet military vehicle exterior color, adopted in the 1930s and used continuously through WWII and the Cold War. The hex value #51653F is based on Vallejo Model Color 70.609 and corroborated by AK Interactive spectrographic measurements of surviving vehicle examples. It appears in soviet_military (green_4bo) and steppe (stop 2).

Cockpit Turquoise

Soviet aircraft cockpits from the 1940s through the 1970s were finished in a distinctive grey-turquoise used across diverse airframes including the MiG-15, MiG-21, Tu-95, and Su-27. The value #5B8F8C is derived from AK Real Colors RC206 (Interior Turquoise Green), formulated from spectrographic analysis of preserved cockpit interiors.

Constructivist Poster Design

Soviet Constructivism (1920s–1930s) developed a visual language centered on a small set of high-contrast colors: red, black, and white, with yellow and blue as secondary elements. The constructivist palette is evocative of this corpus rather than derived from a specific technical standard. The cream (#E8DFC8) reflects the typical ground color of printed poster reproductions and is low-contrast on white backgrounds.
